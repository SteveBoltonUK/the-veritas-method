Partnership Standards

The Foundation Before You Engage

Before any prompt or technique, there are standards that determine whether your AI partnership will produce real value. These are not technical skills — they are human commitments about how you show up. The Partnership Standards ensure your foundation is solid before you engage.

Module Progress

What You’ll Learn

  • How to balance openness to new thinking with a bias for action
  • Why values alignment is essential for responsible, ethical, and sustainable AI use
  • How to distinguish between what you control, what you influence, and what sits outside both
01
Open Mind and Bias for Action
►

This is where the shift begins. On one side, a willingness to think differently — to recognise that patterns and approaches that worked in the past may not always serve you best in a changing environment, and to allow your assumptions to be challenged without becoming defensive.

On the other side, a willingness to act — to move before everything feels fully clear, to test ideas in practice rather than holding them in theory.

Most people naturally lean toward one side. They either stay in learning mode too long, gathering information but not changing behaviour, or they move quickly but without enough reflection. This standard requires both at the same time. Stay open enough to learn, and active enough to make that learning real.

Key Points

  • Willingness to think differently and allow assumptions to be challenged
  • Willingness to act before everything feels fully clear
  • Most people lean toward one side — learning without acting, or acting without reflecting
  • This standard requires both openness and action at the same time

AI does not automatically improve your thinking. It amplifies whatever is already there. If you bring clarity, it becomes clearer. If you bring vagueness, that scales just as effectively.

Try This Prompt
"I want to assess my own balance between openness and action. In my work and life, I tend to lean more toward [learning/thinking mode OR action/doing mode]. Help me identify where this imbalance shows up most, and design one experiment I could run this week that forces me to strengthen the weaker side."
02
Values Aligned
►

Using AI responsibly, ethically, sustainably, and for positive impact.

Responsibility means you remain accountable for outcomes, regardless of how much of the process involves AI. The output is not a decision; it is an input into a decision.

Ethical use means not shaping questions to produce the answer you were hoping for, not presenting AI-generated work as something it is not.

Sustainability operates at two levels: the environmental cost of large-scale AI usage, AND whether your use of AI is strengthening your ability to think over time or quietly weakening it by outsourcing too much cognitive work.

Positive impact is not automatic. It is a choice about what you use this capability for.

Key Points

  • You remain accountable for outcomes — AI output is an input into a decision, not the decision itself
  • Ethical use means honesty in how you frame questions and present AI-generated work
  • Sustainability includes both environmental cost and whether AI is strengthening or weakening your thinking
  • Positive impact requires deliberate choice, not passive assumption

There is a minimum standard here. This is not passive. It requires attention and intent.

Try This Prompt
"Help me create my own 'values alignment checklist' for AI partnership. Based on my values of [YOUR VALUES], what specific commitments should I make about how I use AI? Include commitments about responsibility, ethics, sustainability, and positive impact. Make them concrete enough that I could review them monthly."
03
Agency with Influence
►

Understanding the difference between what you can control, what you can influence, and what sits outside both. Most people spend more time than they realise reacting to the third category.

In AI context: blaming the tool when the output is not useful rather than improving input quality. Deferring decisions because AI appears confident rather than taking responsibility. Waiting for better tools instead of working more effectively with what is available.

Agency is about shifting that orientation. It also includes knowing when NOT to use AI. There are situations where judgement, accountability, or context sit entirely with the human.

Key Points

  • Three circles: what you control, what you influence, and what sits outside both
  • Most people waste energy reacting to what they cannot control
  • In AI context, agency means improving your input rather than blaming the tool
  • Agency includes knowing when NOT to use AI at all

You do not control how quickly AI develops. But you do control how you show up to the partnership. That determines what you get back.

Try This Prompt
"I want to map my current relationship with AI against the three circles: what I control, what I influence, and what is outside both. Help me identify specifically: where am I wasting energy on things I cannot control? Where am I underusing my influence? And where do I need to take more ownership of my decisions rather than deferring to AI?"
